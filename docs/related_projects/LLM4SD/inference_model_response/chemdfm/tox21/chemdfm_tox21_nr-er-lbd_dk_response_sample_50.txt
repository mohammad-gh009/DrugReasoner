 Here are three rules that can be used to predict if a molecule can cause adverse effects based on its structure and properties:
1. The presence of halogenated aromatic compounds: Molecules containing halogens such as chlorine, bromine, or fluorine in an aromatic system have been shown to exhibit estrogenic activity and can potentially cause adverse effects. For example, bisphenol A, a commonly used plasticizer, contains two aromatic rings with chlorine substituents that are known to be estrogenic.
2. The presence of phosphate groups: Phosphate groups can bind to metal ions and form insoluble complexes that can accumulate in the body and cause toxic effects. For example, the use of fertilizers and pesticides containing phosphate compounds has been linked to eutrophication of water bodies and associated health issues in humans and animals.
3. The presence of organic pollutants: Organic pollutants such as pesticides, industrial chemicals, and byproducts of combustion can accumulate in the environment and cause adverse effects in humans and animals. These pollutants can be persistent, bioaccumulative, and toxic (PBT), meaning they tend to accumulate in the food chain and can cause long-term health problems. Examples of PBT organic pollutants include polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs), dichlorodiphenyl trichloroethane (DDT), and polybrominated diphenyl ethers (PBDEs).
